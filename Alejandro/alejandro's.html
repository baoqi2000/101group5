Marine Life Endangerment

The Marine Biology Conservations Society reports that the most common causes for marine life endangerment are loss of habitat, spread of disease, pollution, and unsustainable fishing practices. These each directly impact species that inhabit small, specific environments, such as the zebra shark that inhabit coral reefs off the Australian coast. Threats that plague marine life are difficult to perceive due to the difficulty of perception in comparison to the visibility of land animals.

Vaquita Porpoise

Critically Endangered Species

The Vaquita Porpoise is known as the world's rarest marine animal with only about 10 individuals inhabiting the Gulf of California in Mexico, their native habitat. This species of porpoise was only recently discovered in 1958 and their population has only decreased due to them being caught and drowned in gill nets used by illegal fishing operations.

Hawksbill Turtle

Critically Endangered Species

The Hawksbill Turtle is another marine species that is currently on the critically endangered list. Named for their narrow, pointed beak while also having an overlapping pattern on their shell, giving their scales a serrated look. Originally inhabiting the tropical waters found near coral reefs, these turtles are being sold for their distinctive shell pattern known as a "tortoise shell". Their decreasing numbers are worrying for multiple reasons, but specifically for their link to marine ecosystem health with their contributions toward keeping coral reefs and sea grass beds healthy through their grazing.

Zebra Shark

Endangered Species

While not critically endangered, the zebra shark's decreasing numbers may signal deeper issues within Australia's coral reefs. These sharks tend to stay in shallow waters foraging and hunting for small fish, snails, sea urchins, and crabs. They have also been observed resting on the ocean floor facing the current, having the water pump through their gills allowing them to breathe while they lay down on the floor. They have also been seen "surfing" on the floor, adjusting their fins to adapt to the change in current while also letting them remain still. These gentle giants' biggest threat are humans poaching and hunting them to be sold for their fins, which are a delicacy in many different countries.
